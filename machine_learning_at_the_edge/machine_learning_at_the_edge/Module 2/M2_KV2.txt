
An example of raw data can be seen


in this plot, which illustrates


accelerometer data coming from an


inertial sensor, similar to one


inside smartwatches and smartphones.


The plots show data during several


activities, including walking, running,


climbing up, climbing down, and so on.


A supervised learning system could be


trained to predict the activity from


similar raw data. In Machine Learning,


feature extraction is the process


of deriving calculated values,


known as features, from an initial


set of measured raw data.


Ideally, the chosen features are


informative and non-redundant,


helping the learning model


to make better predictions.


In particular, during feature extraction,


the original data space is


converted into the feature space


which is further processed in


the Machine Learning path. To calculate


any features from this raw data,


it is important to define a window size.


This could range from a few


milliseconds to a few seconds.


It would also be useful to


know the sampling rate,


typically 10 to 100 hertz for such data.


For each window,


the features can be calculated.


There is a huge range of possibilities


for these features and they can be of


several types such as statistical,


temporal and frequency.


For inertial sensors,


the most popular are the area


under the curve, the variance,


the mean, the median —


which is less sensitive


to outliers than the mean —


and the peaks of the fast Fourier transform.


The process of selecting a subset


of the possible features is called


feature selection.


The feature selection can significantly


affect the final results of the


learning model, and for this reason, the


selection is typically made by an excerpt.


The feature set depends greatly


on the application.


For example,


suitable feature sets used for an


audio signal would be very different


from those for network traffic data.


For example,


for audio applications,


the raw data from the microphone


are converted from the time domain


to the frequency space.


The spectogram shown in the


picture is a visual representation


of the spectrum of frequencies of


a signal as it varies with time.


Brighter colors represent a greater


intensity of certain frequencies


measured on the vertical axis at


a certain moment in time.


The Mel-frequency Cepstral Coefficient


is the most widely used


feature in speech and speaker recognition.


The following steps are required


to calculate these.


First,


take a windowed expert of the signal


in order to reduce spectral distortion.


Then perform a fast Fourier


transform on the input speech signal,


to obtain the spectrum.


Calculate the Mel-Frequency bank


energies by integrating the spectral


energy coefficients within triangular


frequency bins arranged uniformly


on the Mel-frequency scale.


Perform the discrete cosine transform on


the logarithms of the filter-bank energies.


This de-correlates the filter-bank


coefficients and yields a compressed


representation of the filter banks.


Finally, append first-order differentials.


How can you classify the data


after feature extraction?


This is the job of the learning model.


For instance,


suppose you had calculated two


features from the accelerometer


data from walking and running,


illustrated here in the two feature space,


a classification model such as


the linear model in the figure,


is able to separate the two


classes in the dataset.


There are many learning models more


complex than this linear model,


to solve complex tasks from activities


recognition to computer vision.


The most popular trend today is


toward Artificial Neural Networks


and Convolutional Neural Networks.
