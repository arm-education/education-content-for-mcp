In the previous video,
we explored how CPUs offer a cost-effective

and power-efficient way to run
AI inference.

But we also hinted at the growing
challenges that come with deploying

modern AI models,
especially generative AI, on CPUs.

In this video,
we'll dig deeper into those challenges

and understand why optimization is so
important.

Let's start with model complexity.

Generative AI models are getting much
bigger.

According to recent research by Jaime
Sevilla and Edu Roldan,

the compute used to train top-tier AI
models is increasing by a factor of 

four to five every single year.

That's exponential growth.

These models now often include tens or
even hundreds of billions of parameters.

Each parameter adds to the computational
workload during inference.

For CPUs, which weren't originally
designed for this kind of scale,

that growth presents a serious hurdle.

You're asking a general-purpose chip to
handle workloads that are constantly

increasing in size and complexity.

Without serious optimization,
the performance gap only gets wider.

Then there's memory-bandwidth.

Even if your CPU can technically perform
the necessary computations,

it still needs to fetch the model weights,
and that means moving data from memory to

the processor.

The larger the model,
the more data needs to be transferred,

and the faster that transfer needs to be.

But memory-bandwidth is limited.

If the processor has to wait for data,
you get bottlenecks.

It's a bit like having a fast car stuck
in a traffic jam,

the engine might be powerful,
but it can't move until the road clears.

And on CPUs,
which have relatively smaller caches and

narrower memory channels compared to GPUs,
this becomes a critical constraint.

This problem is even worse if the model
isn't optimized.

Unnecessary memory reads,
poorly aligned data,

or redundant operations all slow things
down, not just slightly, but dramatically.

Next comes the issue of latency.

In many real-world applications,
speed isn't just a bonus,

it's a necessity.

Take voice assistance,
real-time translation apps,

or interactive chatbots.

These systems need to respond within
milliseconds.

If your model takes too long to generate
a result, the user experience breaks down.

CPUs without acceleration, may struggle to
meet these low latency requirements,

especially if the model is large and the
inference path is unoptimized.

This is one reason why optimizations like
operator fusion, quantization,

and vectorization are so important,

they help squeeze more performance out of
the CPU pipeline and reduce latency.

We also need to talk about scalability.

Working with smaller models is one thing.

Scaling to larger,
more complex models 

introduces new challenges.

As model size grows,
you need to manage thread scheduling,

balance the load, and make sure you're not
wasting cycles waiting for memory.

Efficient multi-threading is tough to get
right,

especially on CPUs with limited parallel
compute resources compared to GPUs.

Finally,
there's the stress on memory hierarchies.

CPUs have multiple layers of cache,
L1,L2,L3,

and optimizing how data moves between
these levels is essential.

If your model constantly accesses main
memory instead of staying within the

cache, you lose performance.

That's why memory-aware scheduling and
layout transformations are becoming such

a big part of CPU-based inference work.

So let's step back and recap.

AI models are growing fast.

That growth puts increasing pressure on
CPU compute capacity, memory-bandwidth,

and response time.

Without optimization, performance suffers.

But if you understand where these
bottlenecks come from,

you can begin to address them.

And that's exactly what we'll look at
next,

how to model AI computations using graphs,
and how that opens the door to powerful

optimization techniques.
