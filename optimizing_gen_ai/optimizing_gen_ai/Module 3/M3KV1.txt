When we think about running AI models,
especially large generative models,

the default assumption is often: "Let's use
a GPU."

And for good reason—

GPUs are powerful,
especially when it comes to handling

large-scale parallel computations.

But here's something surprising: you don't
always need one.

In fact,
optimizing inference to run on CPUs can

offer major advantages in terms of cost,
energy use, and deployment flexibility.

Let's start with cost.

CPUs are already present in nearly every
device—

from smartphones and laptops to edge
gateways and cloud servers.

That means there's no need to purchase
expensive accelerators or build out

specialized infrastructure.

You can leverage the hardware you already
have.

For companies deploying at scale,
that's a huge saving on both capital

expenditure—meaning the upfront cost of hardware—

and operational expenditure—meaning the cost of running and

maintaining it.

Consider an organization rolling out an
AI-powered service 

across thousands of devices.

If each of those devices needs a high-end
GPU,

the costs quickly become astronomical.

But if the same service can run
efficiently on a CPU, using smart

optimizations,
that changes the equation completely.

The deployment becomes affordable and
much more scalable.

Then there's the issue of energy.

GPU's are powerful, yes—but they're also power-hungry.

They consume a lot of electricity,
which not only drives up energy bills,

but also increases the thermal load.

This can require more cooling
infrastructure,

especially in data centers or embedded
environments.

CPUs, on the other hand,
are designed to be more energy efficient.

They can be optimized to scale frequency,
enter low-power states during idle

periods, and run cooler overall.

This matters a lot for edge deployment.

Imagine an AI assistant running on a
mobile phone 

or a voice-controlled system in a car.

These systems need to be fast,
but they also need to conserve battery.

CPU-based inference—especially when paired with low-precision

operations or vectorized execution—strikes a good balance between

performance and power draw.

There's also a privacy angle.

Running inference on-device—locally, on
a CPU—reduces the need to send sensitive

data to external servers.

For applications in healthcare, finance,
or any domain involving personal data,

this has clear advantages.

You improve user privacy,
reduce regulatory complexity,

and avoid potential latency introduced by
uploading and downloading from the Cloud.

Another key reason to optimize for CPU is
ubiquity.

CPUs are already everywhere.

Whether you're deploying to consumer
devices, cloud environments,

or embedded industrial systems,
you can count on a CPU being there.

That means developers can build models
and services that are broadly compatible

and don't require special hardware to
work.

This is particularly useful for
maintaining and scaling systems—

since there's less fragmentation across
deployment environments.

Now,
none of this means CPUs can match GPUs in

raw throughput.

But with the right optimizations—which we'll explore later in this module—

they can deliver excellent performance
for many real-world use cases.

Especially when latency is more important
than massive parallelism,

or when power and cost are limiting
factors.

Let's take an example.

Suppose you're deploying a real-time
translation tool on a smartphone.

You need low-latency responses,
minimal power draw,

and a small memory footprint.

Running inference on a GPU isn't an
option—the device doesn't have one.

But with CPU-friendly optimizations, like
quantization and vectorized execution

with features such as Arm's scalable matrix
extension,

the model runs smoothly and efficiently.

You get high-quality results with no
reliance on cloud connectivity or

external accelerators.

So to summarize:
using CPUs for inference is not just a

fallback—it's often the most practical,
cost-effective, and sustainable choice.

By leveraging existing infrastructure,
reducing energy costs,

and maintaining privacy,
CPU inference unlocks a wide range of 

AI deployments that would otherwise be too
expensive, complex,

or power-hungry to deliver at scale.

make these optimizations increasingly
necessary—

including rising model complexity,
bandwidth limits, and latency demands.
