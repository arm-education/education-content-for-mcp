Let's kick things off by looking at how
Generative AI has exploded into our

everyday world,
and why that growth matters when we're

trying to run these models efficiently.

Today, Generative AI is everywhere.

Think about real-time translators like
TimeKettle's earbuds that can convert

languages on the fly, or Apple's OpenELM — an on-device language model designed to

run directly on your iPhone.

Then there's Perplexity.ai—a Cloud-based system that responds to

user queries like a turbo-charged search
engine.

All of these use Generative AI,
but they're 

running in very different environments.

Some are in the Cloud, backed by huge data
centers.

Others are on Edge devices like
smartphones or IoT devices.

Each computational environment brings its
own advantages and disadvantages.

Running Generative AI in the Cloud seems
easy.

Tons of power, lots of memory,
and scalable infrastructure.

However, Cloud setups have downsides.

They introduce network latency between
the application and the Cloud,

can be costly and raise data privacy
concerns.

On the flip side,
Edge devices give us fast,

localized processing.

You don't need to upload data to a server,
and latency is often much lower.

But Edge devices are constrained,

limited by memory, power, and computational capacity.

That's where things start getting tricky.

Let's talk scale.

In just the past few years,
the size of AI models has exploded.

According to a 2024 report by Jaime
Sevilla and Edu Roldan,

the compute needed to train frontier
models has been growing at a rate of four

to five times per year.

That's exponential growth.

Early models had millions of parameters.

Now we have models with tens or even
hundreds of billions.

These parameters,
which basically represent the models'

capacity,
need to be stored 

and processed during inference.

And this brings us to Sutton's "Bitter
Lesson."

Back in 2019,
Rich Sutton pointed out something key:

many breakthroughs in AI have not stemmed
from complex algorithms,

but rather from scalable ones—those that leverage more compute and data.

As a result,
the trend towards increasingly large and

powerful models is likely to continue.

But here's the catch:

inference—actually running the model—is
increasingly performed on Edge devices

like phones, cars, or drones,
rather than in centralized data centers.

This shift is driven by advantages such
as reduced latency, improved data privacy,

and lower energy costs.

However,
these Edge environments are fundamentally

different from training setups.

They are resource constrained.

Let's say we're deploying a Generative AI
model on an Edge device.

That model might have billions of
parameters.

If it's using 16 bit floating point
precision, every parameter takes two bytes—

so a 70 billion parameter model would
need

140 gigabytes just to store the weights.

Most Edge devices don't even come close
to having that kind of memory.

This means we need to think carefully
about how and where we run these models,

as memory access, power consumption,
and processing speed 

suddenly become major concerns.

And this is what makes optimization so
important.

It's not just about making things faster:
it's about making them possible.

To summarize,
Generative AI is growing rapidly in

capability and complexity.

These models require serious compute and
memory.

Running them on constrained devices
requires a shift in focus—

one that prioritizes efficient inference.

In the next videos,
we'll look at how different device

architectures—CPUs and GPUs—handle these challenges;

and later, we'll explore concrete
techniques to help shrink and speed up

these enormous models, so they can run
smoothly even on tiny devices.
