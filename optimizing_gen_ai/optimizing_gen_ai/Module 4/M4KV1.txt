One of the most effective ways to
optimize AI inference on CPUs is by

reducing how much data we need to compute.

And the simplest way to do that is through quantization.

Quantization involves representing
numbers with fewer bits—

trading some precision for big gains in
performance and memory efficiency.

For example,
instead of using 32-bit floating point

numbers for every weight in a model,
we might switch to 8-bit integers.

That single change cuts memory usage by
75% and reduces the amount of data that

needs to move through memory buses during
inference.

Let's talk about how this works.

In linear quantization, we map the original floating point range

onto an integer range using a scale factor.

For instance,
if your floating point values range

between –1 and 1, and you're converting
them to INT8,

you assign each float a corresponding
integer between –128 and +127.

This process is simple, fast,
and works well for inference—

especially when combined with quantized
matrix multiply instructions like I8MM,

which we'll discuss later.

On Arm CPUs,
we often work with formats like

FP32, FP16, BF16, INT8, and even INT4.

Each step down in bit-width lowers memory
and compute requirements—

but it also reduces numerical precision.

FP16 and BF16 both use 16 bits
but in different ways.

BF16 keeps a larger dynamic range,
which makes it more resistant to

numerical overflow,
which is especially useful for

training or fine-tuning.

But for inference,
INT8 improves efficiency.

It's small, fast,
and accurate enough in most cases—

especially after fine-tuning or
calibration.

If you need even more compression,
INT4 can be used—

although it often comes with a more
noticeable accuracy trade-off and

requires more careful tuning,
even involving techniques such as

quantization-aware training.

Now let's shift gears to another powerful
optimization: pruning.

Pruning is exactly what it sounds
like—cutting away parts of the model that

aren't pulling their weight.

That might mean removing neurons,
channels,

or even individual weights that
contribute

very little to the final output.

The idea is that large models often
contain redundancy—

extra parameters that don't make a
meaningful difference.

By removing these,
we reduce the amount of computation

required during inference.

One common method is called iterative
magnitude pruning.

You start by training a full, dense model.

Then, you remove a small percentage of the
weights with the lowest magnitudes—

the ones closest to zero.

After that,
you fine-tune the model to recover any

accuracy that was lost.

And then you repeat the process,
slowly increasing the sparsity of the
model.

Over time, this creates a model that is smaller,
faster,

and often just as accurate as the
original—

especially for tasks like classification
or translation where robustness is key.

Pruning can be unstructured,
meaning individual weights are removed

regardless of their position in the
matrix.

Or it can be structured, meaning entire rows, columns, or layers are removed

to create a more regular, hardware-friendly shape.

The more structured the pruning,
the easier it is to accelerate on CPUs—

because it aligns better with how memory
is accessed and

how operations are parallelized.

So, to recap:
quantization reduces precision to smaller

bit widths like INT8 or INT4,
drastically reducing

memory use and compute time.

It's particularly effective when matched
with hardware features that support

low-precision arithmetic.

Pruning, on the other hand,
reduces the number of weights and

operations altogether—trimming away unnecessary computations

to speed up the model and lighten its memory
footprint.

Together,
these two techniques can form the

algorithmic foundation of efficient
inference on CPUs.

optimizations—things like loop tiling,
